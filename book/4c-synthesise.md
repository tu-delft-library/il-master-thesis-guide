# 4c. Synthesise

## Introduction
A synthesis is not the same as a summary. When you synthesise information, you add your own thoughts and findings, as well as a comparison of the work of others. You will often be asked to create a synthesis as part of a literature review at the beginning of your project, or at the end of your project by connecting your results to the literature of other scholars.

## Step 1: Organise your Findings

During your master thesis synthesising literature is often necessary at two major points of your process (Victoria University of Wellington, New Zealand, 2022)

1.	Synthesising literature at the start of your project (literature review)
Synthesise literature for a literature review. Often the starting point of a master thesis are the outcomes of a literature review. Based on what others have done, you formulate your own research questions and proposal. This type of synthesis can be done very early in your thesis process. In your final thesis, this type of synthesis often becomes part of your introduction or a separate literature review. Often here you highlight key works and concepts within a field.

2.	Synthesise literature with your own results (writing the discussion or conclusion of your paper)
When you have done your study, you will want to connect your own findings to the main concepts and studies within the field. This synthesis will become part of the discussion and conclusion of your master thesis. Here you connect your findings to the literature to see how they relate, compare how your findings differ and what they add to the body of work that has already been done.

There is not just one standard practice for synthesising literature. It can help to create an overview or matrix with information from your different sources to find similarities and differences (as already mentioned in the previous section about summarising). You can also use a mindmapping tool to help summarise the main findings from the different sources you have studied.
Ask yourself questions like:
-	What major themes or concepts keep coming up in the literature I am reading?
-	What are strengths and weaknesses of my sources?
-	Are there knowledge gaps discussed?
(USU Libraries, 2019)

## Step 2: Decide how to group your sources
When grouping your sources, you integrate the different sources to tell your perspective on the literature you found. Rather than a summary, this is where you combine the sources to create a new overview of the literature on a topic (introduction or literature review), or combine the main findings in the sources with your own results to discuss your findings (discussion).


````{tab-set}

```{tab-item} Methods
If your focus is for example on developing or evaluating a specific method, you could organise your synthesis by methods. You could look at data, equations or models from the sources in your literature review, potentially replot proposed solutions or merge data found by different sources and analyse their findings, connecting it to your own ideas or building your own methodology.

To support these types of syntheses, you might want to look into graph digitization tools like Engauge, which extract data from graphs.

```

``` {tab-item} Themes
When your goal is, for example, to analyse the different discussions going on between scholars, you could use a thematic grouping of sources (College of Arts and Sciences, n.d.).
```

``` {tab-item} Point of view
Point of view can be a helpful format to build a synthesis around when you find many differences between scholars (College of Arts and Sciences, n.d.).
```


``` {tab-item} Chronological

These groupings should help you identify the main ideas within the literature and how they have developed overtime (College of Arts and Sciences, n.d.).

```
````

## Step 3: Write paragraphs with topic sentences
For each paragraph you write, you should have a clear topic sentence and include a few sources supporting this topic sentence. In this way, the paragraphs become the building blocks of your findings within the literature. Note that this topic sentence should reflect your findings and viewpoints in the literature, or connect your own research to the literature. It is not enough to merely provide a summary of the sources. 

If you have your own research findings, use them as part of these paragraphs. How do your findings differ from the already established literature?

Do you want more information on how to build good paragraphs and sentences?
